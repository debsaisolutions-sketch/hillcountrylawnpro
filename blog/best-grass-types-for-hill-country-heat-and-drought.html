Best Grass Types for Hill Country Heat and Drought

Kerrville-area summers are hard on turf. Thin limestone soils, blazing heat, and watering restrictions mean the “pretty lawn” you grew up with elsewhere may not survive here without constant irrigation. Picking a grass built for Central Texas heat and drought is the fastest way to cut water use and keep green coverage through July and August.

What Makes Hill Country Turf Different

Many yards sit on shallow soil over rock, get full sun all day, and compete with oak or cedar shade on the edges. Deer browse, leaf drop, and irrigation limits add stress. Warm-season grasses that go dormant in winter—but stay tough in heat—are usually the better match than cool-season blends sold for northern climates.

Strong Options for This Climate

  • Bermuda: Excellent heat and drought tolerance, recovers from wear, and thrives in full sun. Needs regular mowing and can invade flower beds if edges are not managed.
  • Zoysia: Dense carpet look, good heat tolerance, and moderate drought resistance once established. Slower to fill in than Bermuda, but handles some shade better.
  • St. Augustine: Better shade tolerance than Bermuda, common under tree canopies. Needs more water in extreme drought and can struggle in the hottest, driest full-sun spots.
  • Buffalo grass: Native prairie option that uses little water once established. Lower maintenance look—not a golf-course carpet—but strong for xeriscape-minded yards.

How to Choose for Your Yard

  1. Map sun vs. shade. Full-sun front yards lean Bermuda or Zoysia; heavy oak shade may need St. Augustine or a groundcover mix instead of forcing sun grass.
  2. Be honest about water. If you want low irrigation, favor Bermuda, Zoysia (established), or buffalo—and accept a more natural summer color during drought.
  3. Match use. Kids, dogs, and foot traffic favor Bermuda’s recovery. Formal look and denser feel often point to Zoysia.
  4. Plan establishment. Sod is faster; seed or plugs cost less but need careful watering the first season.

Water-Wise Habits That Matter More Than Brand Hype

Even the right grass fails with daily shallow watering. Deep, infrequent irrigation encourages roots. Mow higher in summer, leave clippings when possible, and avoid fertilizing into heat waves. Pair turf with xeriscaping in hard-to-water zones so you are not fighting rock and slope with a thirsty lawn.
